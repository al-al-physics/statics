Force couple

      We already know that a moment depends on our choice of reference point P. If we take different points of reference, we, in general, obtain different results for the moment \(\vec{M}\). In that case, the moment vector must have its base pinned at the reference point P. It is a fixed vector. Fixed vectors are those which have their base necessarily pinned to a point. By contrast, a free vector does not need to have its base attached to a certain location. So, remember that \(\vec{M}\), in general, is a fixed vector.

      But there is a special and particular case of importance here. Sometimes, in a body, the sum of all forces in a body is zero (yes, the sum of all forces even if they are suffered at different points, we will later see that sometimes we can do this). In those cases, if the total force is zero but the net moment is not zero, such moment can also be called a couple. And couples are very special, because their value is independent from the reference point, so their vector base can be attached anywhere, or in other words, it is a free vector. This result is called the Varignon's Second Moment Theorem.

      We call it couple because it can be expressed as the sum of moments produced by two forces that have opposed lever arms, like:



      We see how the two forces in a couple are equal in modulus and direction but of opposed orientation. The lever arms are also equal in magnitude but opposed.

      Varingon's second theorem is easy to understand. If our total moment is

      \( \vec{M}=\vec{r}_1\times\vec{F}_1+\vec{r}_2\times\vec{F}_2\)

      and now we change our reference point P, we will get a different moment where to each lever arm we need to add a displacement vector \(\Delta\vec{r}\), so

      \(\vec{M}'=\vec{r}'_1\times\vec{F}_1+\vec{r}'_2\times\vec{F}_2\)

      where

      \(\vec{r}'_1=\vec{r}_1+\Delta\vec{r}\)

      \(\vec{r}'_2=\vec{r}_2+\Delta\vec{r}\)

      so we can write

      \(\vec{M}'=(\vec{r}_1+\Delta\vec{r})\times\vec{F}_1+(\vec{r}_2+\Delta\vec{r})\times\vec{F}_2\)

      We can arrange the terms to write

      \(\vec{M}'=\vec{r}_1\times\vec{F}_1+\vec{r}_2\times\vec{F}_2+\)
      \(+\Delta\vec{r}\times(\vec{F}_1+\vec{F}_2)\)

      but we know that \(\vec{F}_1+\vec{F}_2=\vec{0}\) in this case, so we obtain that \(\vec{M}'=\vec{M}\).

      Conclusion: wherever you are given a special moment called couple, you are free to pin its vector wherever you want, because all reference points will give the same total moment.

It is important, then, that you differentiate what is your system and what is not your system. The limits between our system and the 'external world' will be the places where reactions occur.       -angular motion about the contact point.

    Rigid bodies

      A rigid body is one that cannot be deformed. Any distance between each two parts of it must be kept constant at all times. These bodies are very special because they allow to extend the scope of our laws of statics. As you will remember, those laws were valid just for single points. All forces must give zero, but they must be applied to the same suffering point. And all moments must be calculated with respect the same reference point.

      But in the real world, objects are not points, so if we want to make the laws of statics practical, we need to be able to sum forces and moments which are not based at the same point. Let's see a first scenario:



      We have a vertical column with a fixed support, and an vertical external force pointing downwards. Notice that we have switched to a more clean depiction of the force, where the arrow now is displaced for a better visualization, but the little line will always show what is the true base of the vector, the point at which the force is suffered.

      Here, it is clear that the tangent reaction and the moment at the support are both zero, so only the normal reaction will survive in order to have a static situation:



      But you should ask this question here: wasn't illegal to sum forces suffered at different points? The true static equation says that forces must be zero when suffered and added at the same point. However, here the external force is applied at one extreme of the rod while the reaction is suffered at the other extreme. Is this static? The answer is yes, but we need to digg deeper in order to understand what is happening here. Let's slice part of the column in pieces:



      Now let's focus on the upper slice. To be static, it should have the sum of its forces equal to zero. So there has to be an upwards force acting on the bottom of the upper slice:



      So now the first slice is at equilibrium. However, if the green force appears, a reaction must appear as well and will be suffered by the top of the second slice:



      Notice that in the second slice we had to add another force at its bottom in order to have the slice at equilibrium. This will trigger another reaction at the top of the third slice, and so on. The final and global picture will be this:



      What happens here is that each slice of the column is at equilibrium. But the only forces acting on the column are not just the external and the reaction. Many (in fact, infinite) forces appear when we cut (with our imagination) the material. These forces are called internal forces and the produce an axial stress. Axial because it occurs along the axis of the column. And stress because the material is stressed, even if it does not seem it. But if the material would not be rigid, we would notice such stress. In fact, the material can break and this is because the internal stresses that appear as a consequence of the external force applied at the top.

      Conclusion: even if the laws of statics say that the forces to be added must be suffered at the same point, we now see that for a rigid body we can sum all the external forces, even suffered at different points, and equal them to zero. But remember, only external forces, and take into account that reactions are external as well. But the microscopic reality is that equilibrium occurs at each slice, producing an internal stress.

      Notice that when we join the slices again, the internal points at which the internal forces are suffered, which come in pairs, are joined and each pair becomes one again, so at each of these internal points there are two forces suffered, but they will be equal and opposed, so they cancel. So internal forces always cancel and when we analyze only external forces, they don't need to be depicted. They only appear in the drawing when we (imaginarily) break an object to analyze it internally.

      There is a mathematical paradox that occurs here when the slice is reduced to a mathematical point (or line in this case). When the slice has no width at all, let's see what happens:



      The example we had before was a case of compression, but the external force could have been upwards so we would have had a case of traction. When the slices have width it is easy to distinguish the two cases, but notice that as the width becomes zero it becomes harder and harder, in such a way that in the limit of zero width the two bases of the vectors meet at the same point, so the upper and lower versions are the same physical situation depicted differently. The conclusion is that for a mathematical point there is no distinction between compression and traction. Only objects with a finite width can have such distinction. Conclusion: when analyzing a point-like object and considering whether it suffers compression or traction, draw the point with some volume. If you draw a small point, it will be impossible to distinguish the two cases.

      So far we have only analyzed the effect of axial forces, forces that go along the column. Now we will complete our analysis by developing a complementary example:



      Now there will not be a normal reaction at the support. But there will be a horizontal one, this is sure.



      But notice that this column is not at equilibrium, because the forces want to rotate it clockwise. This means that the support will need to perform a reaction moment to compensate it:



      Again, we see that we are adding forces and moments referred to different points, but we already know that we are allowed to do such a thing for rigid bodies. However, it is important to analyze what internal stresses will appear here. Again, we will cut into slices. Let's analyze only the first two upper slices:



      We see that for the upper slice, an interal force pointing to the left is necessary. This will trigger, like before, a chain of internal forces in other slices. But there is something missing: these slices want to rotate clockwise due to the forces, so there have to be some momentum vectors to compensate for this:



      Should we worry about where we should attach the moment symbol? Not at all. Why? Because the moment of the slice produced by the forces is a very special case of moment: a couple, and we already know that for couples the point of application does not matter, they become free vectors. So we draw the counterclockwise moment around the slice, freely. Now the slice is at equilibrium: the forces sum to zero and the moments sum to zero as well. Do you clearly see that moments sum to zero? We only depict one direct moment, but there is another one produced by the couple of forces!

      For the second slice, all forces and moments are internal (green), but the story is the same. Let's analyze the global picture:



      We see that as a consequence of just a single external force, a complex chain reaction of forces and moments is produce to compensate it. The first thing to notice is that internal forces are known as shear, and they make the column suffer in such a way that two consecutive slices want to slide between each other. The second and related stress is the bending moment. This internal stress makes the column to be bent, and in fact, if the column breaks down, it will usually be at the point where bending moment is maximum.

      Again there is a mathematical paradox here. If the slice becomes very thin the points at which the couple of forces produce the internal moment of the slice become too close, so there is a moment, when the slice has zero width, in which there is no shear stress and no bending moment at all. The conclusion, now in general, is that a single point cannot suffer axial stress, shear stress or bending moment. Such stresses are only valid for real objects, with some nonzero volume. So when you draw, for example, a truss or a frame in which there is a joint to be analyzed, draw it with some volume to avoid confussions and paradoxes. All structural objects have volume, so don't worry: all objects will suffer internal stresses.

      If we have the following case



      then you know by eye inspection that this is a sum of the two previous cases, and we will have axial and shear forces and also bending moments. What you need to notice is that while axial stress is independent, shear forces and bending moments are dependent on each other. For now, you will not use these internal stresses, but later in the course you will, so better to understand them now.

      Conclusions: the laws of statics can be applied to rigid bodies without worrying if we sum forces suffered by different points. In this case, we can only consider external forces. Moments can also be added without worrying the mixture of reference points.

    Rods

      There is a special case that is worth to be analyzed in detail. Many times, your structures will be composed of just rigid rods and articulated joints. An articulated joint is like a hinge support: they forbid x and y displacement but they allow rotations:



      It is of the uttermost importance that you notice that joints, or articulated points, cannot resist rotation, so they cannot exhibit a moment reaction. This has a very important consequence when we separate the rods for analysis:

But here comes the important point: if the joints cannot provide moment and if the structure is to be static, no moments can be produced by the forces! And the only way in which this can be true is this:



      In other words: rods can only experience axial stresses, and they can be under traction or compression. In the picture our hypothesis are all depicted as tractions, but it does not matter which one you pick. What is crucial here is that, even if the joint gives a reaction with two unknowns, you need to see that the absence of reaction moment reduces the number of unknowns to one, because the direction of the force will be the same as the direction of the rod, so there will be just one unknown!

      Don't analyze too much this picture because it is not supported anywhere, so if you try to sum all the forces at each joint and equal them to zero you will soon arrive to the conclusion that all the rods suffer zero force. That is why this structure is floating in the air, without weight or supports! What happens if we really put this structure on a pair of supports? Think about it: if the supports are of movable or hinge type, both allow rotations, so they will act like joints in this sense, and all the rods will be either under traction or compression (or null force). However, if there is a fixed support, the rods that are touching this support will suffer a moment in their supported extremes, so at the other extremes they will need to suffer moments as well, which will be chain-propagated to many, if not all, the rest of the rods. So watch out: the rule that rods have their internal forces along the direction of their own bodies is only valid when the rods don't suffer moments!
