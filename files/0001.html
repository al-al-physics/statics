    Basic trigonometry

      Let's begin by recalling the definitions of the three basic trigonometric functions: the sine, the cosine and the tangent.



      This is a right-angled triangle, which means that one angle has \(90^o\). Only when we have such a triangle, we can apply Pythagoras' theorem,

      \(c^2=a^2+b^2\)

      The following definitions are, like Pythagoras' theorem, only valid for right-angled triangles. We must know that

      \( \sin{\alpha}=\frac{a}{c} \)

      \( \cos{\alpha}=\frac{b}{c} \)

      \( \tan{\alpha}=\frac{a}{b} \)

      where \(a\) is the opposite side (or opposite cathetus) and \(b\) the adjacent side (or adjacent cathetus) with respect to \(\alpha\). By contrast, \(a\) is the adjacent side and \(b\) the opposite side with respect to \(\beta\). In both cases \(c\) is the hypotenuse. Notice in the figure that the right angle (the one having \(90^o\) is marked with a little rectangle. Always mark with this sign all the right angles you encounter, it will be very helpful.

      Notice as well that the tangent is simply the sine divided by the cosine,

      \( \tan{\alpha}=\frac{\sin{\alpha}}{\cos{\alpha}}=\frac{a/c}{b/c}=\frac{a}{b}. \)

      One more thing you need to know is that all triangles, right-angled or not, must hold the following theorem:

      The sum of all three angles gives \(180^o\)

      In a right-angled triangle like the one in the figure, if we know \(\alpha\) and we know that there is one angle of \(90^o\), then we know that \(\beta=90-\alpha\). You will use this result many times, so try to understand it well, and see how it is a consequence of the previous theorem.

      Could we define trigonometric functions for the angle \(\beta\)? Of course:

      \( \sin{\beta}=\frac{b}{c} \)

      \( \cos{\beta}=\frac{a}{c} \)

      \( \tan{\beta}=\frac{b}{a} \)

      There are many important relations for the trigonometric functions. However, in this course you may only need the following:

      \( \sin^2x+\cos^2x=1 \)

      \( \sin{(-x)}=-\sin{(x)} \)

      \( \cos{(-x)}=\cos{(x)} \)

      \( \tan{(-x)}=-\tan{(x)} \)

      What goes inside a trigonometric function is always an angle. In \(\cos{x}\), \(x\) is an angle. Now, there are two ways of doing this. In the calculator you usually have (at least) two modes: DEG and RAD. The first, DEG, means you are measuring angles in degrees, while the second, RAD, means that you are measuring angles in radians. Angles in degrees are such that a right angle is 90 and a whole turn is 360. By contrast, a whole turn in radians is \(2\pi\), half a turn is \(\pi\) and a right angle is \(\pi/2\). If you need to convert degrees into radians or the opposite way, just remember one of these rules to establish a simple relation. For example, let's say you want to know 30 degrees into radians, so you write

      \(\frac{\pi}{180}=\frac{x}{30}\)

      and then you isolate x, so you get the desired angle in radians. The rule that $\pi$ is $180^o$ is probably the most simple. A common source of errors comes from the usual mistaking of which angle mode your calculator is operating. To practice this, calculate the following expressions:

      Once we are equipped with all these trigonometric tools, we can have different scenarios when presented with a right-angled triangle: We can have 2 sides of the right-angled triangle and we need the third side: in those cases we apply Pythagoras' theorem. If you are given two angles and no sides, you need to apply the sines or the cosines theorems, which are not necessary in this course but they are very important. But most typically you will be given a side and an angle, or just two sides, so you will use the definitions of the three basic trigonometric functions (sin, cos and tan) to extract the information you want. Further problems will give you this experience.

      The sines theorem says that in any triangle, the ratio between the sine of each angle and the length of its opposite side is always equal, so for each triangle we can create three such ratios and produce equalities. This will be needed in some problems in the future, and it can save your day when you are faced with a triangle that is not right-angled!
